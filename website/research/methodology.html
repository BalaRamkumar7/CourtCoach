How We Fact-Check Every Metric

CourtCoach turns body angles into coaching. This paper documents how we validate every target range against the evidence — the measurement model, the verdict rubric, and the honest reasons most metrics land as estimates rather than proven numbers.

The method

Every metric was put through the same four steps. The goal was never to make the app look finished — it was to know, honestly, which numbers the evidence supports and which are educated defaults.

01

Define what's measured

CourtCoach reads the interior joint angle from pose tracking — 180° is a fully straight limb, smaller means more bend — which matches the convention used in the biomechanics literature, so published numbers are directly comparable. Metrics like Balance, Release Height, Hand Position and Body Lean are 0–100 app scores with no physical unit.

02

Gather the evidence

Three parallel research passes — one per skill — pulled 24 usable sources: 8 peer-reviewed studies, 2 academic papers, and 14 coaching resources. Each source was read for what it actually measures, and sources that couldn't be verified were dropped (one fabricated figure was caught and discarded).

03

Assign a verdict

Each metric's target range was compared against the evidence and given one of four verdicts (below). Where the range was wrong, we corrected it in the app; where no research exists, we labeled it an estimate rather than dressing it up as fact.

04

Apply and document

All corrections in this paper are already live in the CourtCoach build — the tables below show final values, not proposals. This document is the permanent record of why each threshold is what it is.

Two limits that touch every number

Phase sampling. Metrics are averaged across the whole rep, not isolated to the instant of release — so an angle blends the set-point, the release and the follow-through, which can differ by 70–100°. And app scores (Balance, Release Height, Hand Position, Body Lean) have no real-world unit, so no study can confirm a specific threshold. Both are honest constraints, noted wherever they matter.

The verdict rubric

Confirmed

The current range matches peer-reviewed measurements. Kept as-is.

Corrected

The range or label was wrong or contradicted the evidence, and has been fixed in the app.

Estimate

No hard research exists — an app score, or an under-studied skill. The threshold is an engineering default backed by coaching cues.

Redefined

The metric didn't make sense for the drill and was replaced with one that does.

Why "Estimate" is the majority verdict

32 of 44 metrics are estimates — and that's by design, not by failure. Balance, Release Height, Hand Position and Body Lean are invented 0–100 scores no study can grade, and passing and dribbling are genuinely under-researched compared to shooting. An estimate here is an honestly-labeled threshold built from coaching consensus, ready to be upgraded the moment better data exists. Calling it anything more certain would be the dishonest choice.

Sources

24 cited · 1 discarded

Shooting — S1–S10

  1. Biomechanical characteristics of proficient free-throw shooters (Frontiers / PMC10436204)Study
    Elbow/knee angle at prep vs. release, proficient vs. non-proficient, markerless mocap.
  2. Impact of Distance and Proficiency on Shooting Kinematics (PMC9590067)Study
    Prep-phase elbow/knee angles by distance and proficiency; confirms the angle convention.
  3. Biomechanics of the Basketball Jump Shot (Physiopedia)Wiki
    Elbow/knee flexion during prep; release-at-peak-jump guidance.
  4. The Overlooked Importance of Arm & Wrist Angles (Breakthrough Basketball)Coaching
    Wrist snap / follow-through coaching cues.
  5. Arm Joint Coordination When Shooting Behind the 3-Point Line (PMC12121896)Study
    Shoulder→elbow→wrist coordination for 3-point shooting.
  6. Quantifying Stability in Basketball (Coach Dave Love)Coaching
    Base-of-support / stance-width basis for the Balance metric.
  7. Hal Wissel's Basketball Shooting Mechanics (Coach's Clipboard)Coaching
    B.E.E.F. framework; elbow-alignment guidance.
  8. Ankle biomechanics of the three-step layup (PMC10618240)Study
    Confirms layup research targets landing mechanics, not shooting-form angle.
  9. Effects of Muscle Fatigue on Lower Extremity Biomechanics During the Layup (MDPI Biomechanics 2025)Study
    Knee mechanics vary non-monotonically across layup takeoff/landing phases.
  10. Biomechanical Analysis of the Jump Shot in Basketball (Struzik et al., Journal of Human Kinetics, 2014)Study
    Jump-shot lower-limb power and landing forces >5× body weight. Measures power, not joint angles.

Passing — P1–P7

  1. Influence of Fatigue on Kinematic Parameters of Basketball Passing (IJERPH 2021)Study
    Only passing-specific peer-reviewed study; reports angular velocity, not release-angle degrees.
  2. Influence of Athletic Stance Height/Width on Response Time in Basketball (ResearchGate)Study
    General stance knee-flexion data — closest proxy for passing knee-bend plausibility.
  3. Basketball Passing Skill: Bent Elbow Pass (PGC Basketball)Coaching
    Standard chest pass ends in a full-extension delivery point.
  4. Chest Pass in Basketball: General Information (HoopStudent)Coaching
    Elbows straighten from a bent start at release.
  5. Body Position for Passing (Sportplan)Coaching
    Generic "bend your knees" cue applied undifferentiated across pass types.
  6. Master How to Throw Overhead Pass Basketball (HoopsKing)Coaching
    Overhead-pass elbow/knee cues; basis for the knee-range correction.
  7. The Outlet Pass: A Basketball Coach's Guide (TheHoopsGeek)Coaching
    Leg-power generation for the overhead/outlet pass — no straighter-stance mention.

Dribbling — D1–D7

  1. Basketball Defense: 10 Keys to a Great Defensive Stance (Breakthrough Basketball)Coaching
    Generic athletic/defensive stance cues, no degree figures.
  2. Beginner Drills To Develop An Effective Crossover Move (Breakthrough Basketball)Coaching
    Crossover stance depth, ball-below-knees cue.
  3. How to Do a Crossover Dribble (XbotGo)Coaching
    Crossover stance, ball height, lean/shoulder-drop misdirection cue.
  4. Basketball Dribbling Drills: 15 Drills for Every Level (joinstriveon.com)Coaching
    USA Basketball "waist-high or lower" cue; stationary-drill knee-depth correction.
  5. Fundamentals of Good Basketball Defensive Stance and Footwork (Sporator)Coaching
    The one explicit generic stance figure (~45° knee bend), used as a reference point.
  6. Biomechanical Study on a Basketball Dribble with a Cutting Motion (ISBS / Uni Konstanz)Paper
    Peer-reviewed crossover-type study; measures center-of-gravity velocity, not joint angles.
  7. Kinematic Analysis of Low Dribble at Execution Phase (Pandey & Patel, Golden Research Thoughts, 2012)Paper · low-rigor
    Only source with a measured dribble knee angle (103.85° ± 18.95°, n=20). Non-indexed journal — weak support only.
